Read: Comparing Energy Efficiency Strategies – Lessons from Sweden for Nigeria

Published: June 2025 • 6 min read

Sweden shows a very compelling case of how high energy consumption does not have to translate into high emissions. As the article explains, Sweden consumes large amounts of energy per capita; however, it also maintains low carbon emissions through deliberate energy choices which are rooted in renewables and nuclear power. By contrast, Nigeria’s energy system is limited by inefficiencies, reliance on fossil fuel, and underutilization of available renewable resources, despite vast natural potential in the country.

One of Sweden’s strongest advantages is its diversified renewable mix: hydropower, bioenergy, wind, and nuclear energy which collectively account for nearly all electricity generation. In 2023, “98.5 per cent of Sweden’s electricity came from renewable sources or nuclear power” (Swedish Institute, 2024). This contrasts sharply with Nigeria’s reality, where electricity generation relies heavily on gas-fired turbines and coal plants. Frequent grid failures force households and businesses to depend on private generators; an outcome that is costly, inefficient, and polluting! Nigeria’s energy choices therefore increase emissions while also delivering unreliable supply.

Furthermore, another area where Sweden excels is policy design. The Electricity Certificate System obliges retailers to purchase green electricity, while producers are incentivized to generate renewable power. Nigeria lacks that type of coherent market-based framework; although renewable energy policies exist, enforcement is weak, and investment security remains quite low, solar and wind projects are often small-scale and fragmented (IEA, 2023).

To advise Nigeria’s government from an efficiency standpoint, as an Energy Efficiency Analyst myself, three priorities stand out. First, Nigeria should aggressively scale decentralized renewables, particularly solar mini-grids; to serve rural and peri-urban communities (this would reduce generator usage). Second, policy incentives similar to Sweden’s certification system should be introduced, encouraging utilities and private investors to prioritize renewables rather than fossil fuels. Third, Nigeria must invest in energy efficiency technologies such as heat pumps and combined heat-and-power systems; these are largely absent today, yet they could significantly reduce wasted energy across industries.

In conclusion, Nigeria’s challenge is not resource scarcity – it is governance and execution. As Sweden demonstrates, strategic planning, long-term policy consistency, and investment in renewables can deliver reliable energy with low emissions; Nigeria can follow this path if reforms are taken seriously.

Read: The Green Index Initiative – Benchmarking Urban Sustainability in Africa

Published: March 2025 • 10 min read

Urban centers across Africa are facing mounting pressure from rapid population growth, rising energy demand, and the accelerating impacts of climate change. With Africa’s urban population projected to double by 2050, the urgency for sustainable planning has never been greater. Cities are engines of economic growth, yet they are also becoming major sources of carbon emissions, waste generation, and energy consumption.

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 7, 11, and 13 emphasize clean energy access, resilient cities, and climate action. However, policymakers often lack reliable tools to measure progress at the city level. This data gap weakens accountability and slows evidence-based decision-making.

The Green Index Initiative responds to this challenge by offering a quantitative framework to benchmark urban sustainability across African cities. It evaluates performance using indicators such as energy access, renewable adoption, transport systems, waste management, air quality, and climate resilience.

By aggregating data from national bureaus of statistics, international development agencies, and city-level datasets, the initiative ensures that assessments are both credible and comparable. This methodological rigor allows stakeholders to move beyond anecdotal evidence and base policy decisions on transparent metrics that reflect real urban conditions.

Importantly, the Green Index does not seek to rank cities for competition alone, but to foster learning and collaboration. High-performing cities serve as reference points, enabling peer-to-peer knowledge sharing and the transfer of best practices across regions.

Through this approach, the initiative empowers local governments to identify priority areas for investment, from clean transport systems to improved waste management and renewable energy infrastructure. Over time, this data-driven feedback loop strengthens urban governance and accelerates progress toward Africa’s sustainable development goals.

Key Insights

Urban Sustainability Is Uneven Across Cities
The Green Index analysis reveals significant disparities in sustainability performance across African cities. While some urban centers show strong progress in renewable energy adoption, public transportation systems, and waste management, others continue to struggle with basic infrastructure and service delivery. This uneven development highlights the urgent need for targeted, city-specific interventions rather than one-size-fits-all policies. Local context, governance capacity, and investment priorities play a critical role in determining sustainability outcomes.

Energy Access and Transport Drive Overall Scores
Energy reliability and transportation efficiency emerged as the strongest determinants of city sustainability rankings. Cities with higher renewable penetration, stable power supply, and integrated transport systems consistently scored better across multiple indicators. Conversely, cities reliant on fossil fuels and informal transport networks recorded higher emissions and congestion levels. This underscores the importance of transitioning to clean energy and investing in mass transit systems as core strategies for sustainable urban development.

Data-Driven Governance Improves Climate Resilience
Cities that demonstrated consistent data collection and monitoring frameworks showed greater resilience to climate-related risks such as flooding, heatwaves, and air pollution. Evidence-based policymaking enabled quicker response times and more effective urban planning decisions. The findings suggest that strengthening municipal data systems and promoting open-data policies can significantly enhance adaptive capacity, improve accountability, and accelerate progress toward SDGs 7, 11, and 13.

Conclusion

The Green Index Initiative demonstrates the power of data in shaping sustainable urban futures across Africa. By providing a standardized, evidence-based framework, it enables governments, development partners, and city planners to identify performance gaps, prioritize interventions, and track progress over time. This approach moves sustainability discourse beyond political commitments into measurable action, ensuring that climate strategies are grounded in real outcomes.

The findings reveal that strong policy frameworks, renewable energy integration, and efficient urban planning significantly influence city performance. Cities such as Rabat, Tunis, and Kigali show that deliberate investment in green infrastructure, waste management systems, and clean mobility can deliver tangible environmental benefits. These success stories offer replicable models for other African cities seeking to accelerate their sustainability transitions.

Looking ahead, the Green Index Initiative will continue to evolve by incorporating more cities, expanding indicator coverage, and strengthening partnerships across the continent. As climate risks intensify, this tool will remain critical in guiding inclusive, data-driven urban development. Ultimately, sustainable African cities are achievable—but only through coordinated action, transparent governance, and long-term commitment to evidence-based policy making.
